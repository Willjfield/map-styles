Accessibility:

NEGATIVE

This change set contains several improvements but is ultimately net negative for accessibility due to critical failures in a few areas that would substantially impair usability for many users.

---

## What Improves

**Text contrast (most labels):** Moving place, POI, road, country, state, and continent text colors to `#000000` on a white (`#ffffff`) background is a clear improvement. Pure black on white achieves a contrast ratio of 21:1, well above the **WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum)** threshold of 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG21/#contrast-minimum).

**Text halo opacity:** Increasing halo opacity from 0.7–0.8 to 0.9 for most labels slightly improves the protective buffer around text, which helps legibility over varied backgrounds. This supports the intent of **WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3**.

**Road label halo:** Adding a halo (`rgba(255,255,255,0.9)`) where previously there was none, and increasing halo width from 1 to 1.5, is a clear improvement. The prior state had no halo at all, meaning dark road label text competed directly with line colors beneath it. This change aids readability per **WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3** and general cartographic legibility practice.

---

## Critical Failures

### 1. Water labels lose all legibility — severe contrast failure

**water_name_line and water_name_point:**
- Text color: `#5d60be` → `#ffffff` (white)
- Halo color: `rgba(255,255,255,0.7)` → `rgba(0,0,0,0)` (fully transparent — halo removed)
- Halo width: `1` → `0`

Water is now `#555555`. White text (`#ffffff`) on `#555555` yields a contrast ratio of approximately **4.6:1**, which barely passes **WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3** in isolation. However, the halo has been **completely removed** (opacity 0, width 0). Without a halo, water labels render directly over the water fill with no protective buffer. At tile boundaries or near coastlines, labels will partially render over the white background (`#ffffff`), where white-on-white is **1:1 contrast — a complete failure**.

The previous design (`#5d60be` text with a white halo on light-blue `#a0c8f0` water) was more robust: the halo ensured legibility regardless of what the text overlapped.

**Guideline:** WCAG 2.1 SC 1.4.3 Contrast (Minimum); also relevant is **W3C WAI – Understanding SC 1.4.3** which notes that text must meet contrast requirements against all backgrounds it may appear over (https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG21/Understanding/contrast-minimum).

**Alternatives:**
- Keep water label text as dark (`#000000` or `#1a1a1a`) with a semi-opaque white halo (`rgba(255,255,255,0.85)`, width 1.5) so it remains legible over the dark water and any adjacent background.
- Or retain a colored text (e.g., a dark navy `#1a237e`) with a white halo of width ≥ 1, which was the prior approach and is conventional for hydrographic labels in cartographic practice.

---

### 2. Water color change removes semantic differentiation — functional accessibility regression

Water: `rgb(158,189,255)` → `#555555` (dark gray)

This is a dramatic desaturation. Water is now the **same neutral gray family as roads, buildings, and rail**. For users with **color vision deficiency (CVD)**, particularly deuteranopia or protanopia, color is already a limited channel. Stripping the blue hue from water removes one of the most universally recognizable map conventions — blue = water — that even color-blind users typically rely on because blue-yellow discrimination is largely preserved across most CVD types.

**Guideline:** **W3C WAI – Cognitive Accessibility**
